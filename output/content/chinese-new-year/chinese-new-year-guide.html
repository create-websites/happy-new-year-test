A hands-on guide to the Spring Festival, honoring family, luck, and new beginnings tied to the Lunar Calendar.

The Core Meaning of Spring Festival

A Festival of Renewal and Family

The **Chinese New Year**, also known as the **Spring Festival (Chūnjié)**, is the most significant celebration in China and East Asia. It signifies the shift from winter to spring and emphasizes family reunions and honoring ancestors. Spanning 15 days, it concludes with the **Lantern Festival**. The highlight is the **Reunion Dinner** on New Year's Eve, where families make every effort to come together. Every custom, from thorough house cleaning to wearing red, is aimed at **banishing bad luck** and **inviting good fortune** (Fú) and prosperity for the year ahead.

The Lunar Calendar and Chinese Zodiac

Determining the Date and Sign

The Chinese New Year date is based on the **Lunarsolar Calendar**, occurring on the new moon between January 21st and February 20th. It ushers in a year named after one of the 12 animals in the **Chinese Zodiac**. This zodiac follows a 12-year cycle, with each animal (Rat, Ox, Tiger, Rabbit, Dragon, Snake, Horse, Goat, Monkey, Rooster, Dog, Pig) representing a year. It's thought that a person's birth year animal influences their traits and fate. The celebration often takes the name of the incoming year's animal (e.g., Year of the Dragon), shaping the festival's theme and decorations.

The Essential Family Traditions

These 15-day customs are essential for bringing fortune and success in the new year.

1. Grand Cleaning (Sǎochén)

The whole house is thoroughly cleaned *before* New Year's to remove bad luck and welcome good fortune. Cleaning is forbidden on New Year's Day.

2. Reunion Dinner (Tuányuán Fàn)

The year's key feast on New Year's Eve features dishes like dumplings (Jiǎozi) and spring rolls (Chūnjuǎn), symbolizing fortune and success.

3. Red Envelopes (Hóngbāo)

Red envelopes with money are gifted by married couples or elders to children or unmarried juniors, symbolizing fortune and warding off evil spirits.

4. Lion Dances & Firecrackers

Noisy firecrackers and lively lion or dragon dances drive off the mythical Nian and dispel bad luck, cleansing the air for the new year.

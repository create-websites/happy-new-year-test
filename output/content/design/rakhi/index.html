The modern meaning of the thread

Raksha Bandhan is still rooted in love—its circle of belonging is simply growing.

Raksha Bandhan has always been a festival of relationships. Traditionally, it is associated with a sister tying a rakhi around her brother’s wrist and the brother promising to protect her. That familiar ritual continues to hold deep emotional value for millions of families.

Yet relationships have changed, families have evolved and ideas of protection have become more equal. Today, Raksha Bandhan is increasingly being celebrated not only as a brother-sister festival, but as a broader expression of mutual care, emotional support, gratitude and responsibility.

Sisters are tying rakhis to sisters. Brothers are exchanging rakhis with one another. Friends are recognizing chosen-family bonds. Children are tying rakhis to pets. Environmentally conscious families are choosing rakhis made from seeds, natural fibers and biodegradable materials.

The sacred thread remains, but its meaning is becoming wider and more inclusive.

Chapter 01

Partnership

From one-way protection to mutual respect, support and responsibility.

01

The Evolving Bond: From Protection to Partnership

The traditional message of Raksha Bandhan is often described in simple terms: a sister prays for her brother’s well-being, and the brother promises to protect her.

This idea emerged in societies where women often had limited financial independence, moved away from their parental homes after marriage and depended heavily on male relatives during times of difficulty. A brother’s promise of protection could therefore represent real social and economic support.

In modern families, however, sibling relationships are rarely so one-directional.

Sisters protect brothers emotionally. They offer advice, provide financial support, help care for parents and stand beside their siblings during illness, career challenges, relationship problems and personal crises.

Brothers may protect their sisters not by controlling their decisions, but by respecting their independence, supporting their ambitions and standing against discrimination or mistreatment.

The modern spirit of Raksha Bandhan is therefore moving from protection to partnership.

The promise is no longer simply:

“

I will protect you.

It is increasingly:

“We will support one another.”

“We will stand together during difficult times.”

“We will respect each other’s choices.”

“We will remain connected even when life takes us in different directions.”

This shift does not weaken tradition. It strengthens the ethical meaning of the festival by making responsibility mutual.

02

What Protection Means Today

In contemporary relationships, protection should not mean possession, authority or control.

A brother does not protect his sister by restricting where she goes, whom she meets or what career she chooses. A sister does not support her brother by ignoring unhealthy behavior or shielding him from accountability.

Healthy protection means creating a relationship in which both people feel respected and safe.

It may involve:

  • Defending a sibling when they are treated unfairly
  • Supporting their education or career
  • Helping during a financial emergency
  • Sharing responsibility for aging parents
  • Respecting personal boundaries
  • Listening without judgment
  • Encouraging professional help during emotional or medical difficulties
  • Celebrating success without competition

Raksha Bandhan can become an annual opportunity for siblings to ask a more meaningful question:

“

How can I support you better this year?

That question transforms the festival from a symbolic ritual into an active commitment.

Inclusive Bonds

Celebrating siblings, sisters-in-law, chosen family and every modern family structure.

03

Sisters for Sisters: Celebrating Female Solidarity

One of the most meaningful modern developments is the growing practice of sisters tying rakhis to one another.

For many women, sisters are their earliest friends, closest confidantes and lifelong support systems. They share childhood memories, family responsibilities, personal fears and major life transitions.

An older sister may act as a mentor, protector and guide. A younger sister may bring emotional strength, companionship and a fresh perspective. Sisters may support one another through education, marriage, motherhood, divorce, career changes, illness or the loss of family members.

A sister tying a rakhi to another sister recognizes this bond openly.

It says:

“You have protected me too.”

“You have been present when I needed someone.”

“Our relationship deserves to be celebrated.”

This practice also challenges the assumption that protection must always come from a man. Women have always protected, advised, defended and supported one another. The modern sister-to-sister rakhi gives visible form to that reality.

04

Rakhi Between Sisters-in-Law

Raksha Bandhan is also expanding to include relationships between sisters-in-law.

In some communities, especially in Rajasthan and among Marwari families, a lumba rakhi is traditionally tied to the bangle of a brother’s wife. The custom acknowledges that the sister-in-law is an important part of the family bond.

Modern families are taking this idea further. Women may exchange rakhis with sisters-in-law as a sign of friendship, mutual respect and shared responsibility within the family.

This can be especially meaningful because relationships between women in extended families are often represented through stereotypes of competition or conflict. A rakhi can offer a different message—one of cooperation and solidarity.

It can symbolize a promise to support one another rather than compete for attention, influence or approval.

05

Brothers Tying Rakhis to Brothers

Raksha Bandhan can also be celebrated between brothers.

Brothers may share a deep emotional connection but often grow up in environments where affection between men is expressed indirectly. A rakhi exchange can create space for brothers to acknowledge gratitude, loyalty and mutual responsibility.

One brother may have supported another through school, employment, illness or financial difficulty. They may jointly care for parents or help each other navigate major life decisions.

Exchanging rakhis allows them to say something that may otherwise remain unspoken:

“I value your presence in my life.”

“I will stand beside you.”

“You do not have to face everything alone.”

This does not replace the traditional brother-sister ritual. It expands the festival’s central principle: meaningful relationships deserve acknowledgment.

06

Celebrating Chosen Family

Not everyone grows up with a biological brother or sister. Others may be separated from their siblings by distance, conflict or loss.

For such individuals, close friends, cousins, mentors, neighbors and colleagues may become chosen family.

Raksha Bandhan has long included the possibility of creating symbolic sibling relationships. A rakhi can formally recognize a bond based not on blood, but on trust and consistent care.

A friend who supported someone through grief may feel more like a sibling than a distant relative. A cousin raised in the same household may share a sibling-like connection. A mentor may provide years of guidance and protection.

Celebrating chosen family makes Raksha Bandhan more inclusive while remaining faithful to its deeper meaning.

However, the gesture should always be sincere and mutually comfortable. A rakhi should not be used casually to impose a relationship or create an obligation the other person has not accepted.

07

Inclusive Raksha Bandhan for Every Family

Modern families take many forms. Some are single-parent households. Some include adopted children, step-siblings, half-siblings or blended families. Others are built through friendship and community.

Raksha Bandhan can offer space for all these relationships.

A stepbrother and stepsister may use the festival to strengthen a new family bond. Adopted siblings may celebrate the love that has grown through shared life rather than shared biology. Cousins raised together may recognize one another as brothers and sisters.

The festival becomes more relevant when it welcomes the reality of modern family life instead of limiting participation to one traditional family structure.

Compassion

Including pets and animal welfare while keeping safety and comfort first.

08

The Pet-Friendly Rakhi

For many households, pets are not simply animals. They are family members.

Dogs, cats, rabbits and other companion animals provide affection, comfort, routine and emotional support. They greet family members without judgment, remain present during difficult periods and often become an important part of childhood memories.

It is therefore unsurprising that some families now celebrate pet-friendly Raksha Bandhan.

Children may tie a soft decorative rakhi to a pet’s collar or place it loosely around the animal for a brief photograph. Families may prepare pet-safe treats and thank their companion for the joy and comfort it brings.

The message is simple:

“You protect us emotionally.”

“You bring love into our home.”

“You are part of our family.”

09

Safety Comes First

Pet-friendly Raksha Bandhan should always prioritize the animal’s comfort and safety.

Traditional rakhis may contain beads, glitter, bells, artificial flowers, metal pieces or loose threads that can be swallowed. Some may use adhesives, dyes or fragrances that irritate an animal’s skin.

A safer pet rakhi should be:

  • Lightweight
  • Soft and nonrestrictive
  • Free from small detachable pieces
  • Made without glitter, sharp decorations or toxic glue
  • Attached to the collar rather than tied tightly around the neck or leg
  • Removed immediately if the animal appears uncomfortable

The rakhi should never interfere with breathing, movement or circulation.

Pet owners should also avoid feeding animals traditional sweets. Chocolate, excessive sugar, raisins and some nuts can be harmful to pets. A veterinary-approved treat is a much safer way to include them in the celebration.

A good pet-friendly celebration is short, gentle and centered on the animal’s well-being rather than the perfect photograph.

10

Rakhi for Animal Welfare

Some families are extending the pet-friendly theme beyond their own homes.

Instead of purchasing expensive gifts, siblings may donate to an animal shelter, sponsor a rescued animal or provide food and supplies to a local welfare organization.

Children can be encouraged to prepare water bowls for street animals, support vaccination drives or learn how to behave safely and compassionately around animals.

This gives the festival a larger purpose. The idea of protection becomes a commitment to care for vulnerable living beings.

Chapter 04

A Greener Thread

Plantable, handmade, edible and reusable ideas that reduce festival waste.

11

Going Green: The Environmental Cost of Decorative Rakhis

Modern commercial rakhis are often made with plastic, synthetic thread, glitter, foam, metal foil and non-biodegradable decorations.

They may look attractive for a few hours but remain in the environment for years. Small plastic components can enter drains, rivers and soil. Glitter can break into microplastic particles. Excessive packaging creates additional waste.

When millions of families celebrate the festival at the same time, even small disposable items can have a significant cumulative impact.

Eco-friendly Raksha Bandhan begins by asking a simple question:

What happens to the rakhi after the celebration?

A sustainable rakhi should either be reusable, biodegradable, compostable or capable of creating something beneficial after use.

12

Plantable Rakhis: A Thread That Becomes a Plant

Plantable rakhis are among the most creative environmentally friendly options.

These rakhis contain seeds embedded in biodegradable paper, clay or another natural material. After the festival, the decorative portion can be planted in soil. With proper watering and sunlight, the seeds may grow into herbs, flowers or vegetables.

Common seed choices include:

  • Basil or tulsi
  • Marigold
  • Coriander
  • Mustard
  • Fenugreek
  • Tomato
  • Spinach
  • Wildflowers

The concept is especially meaningful because the rakhi continues to represent care after it leaves the wrist.

The sibling bond becomes a living plant that must be watered, protected and allowed to grow.

This creates a powerful metaphor: relationships flourish when they receive regular attention.

13

How to Plant a Seed Rakhi

Instructions vary depending on how the rakhi is made, but the general process is simple.

First, remove any non-biodegradable thread, clasp or decoration. Soak the seed-paper or seed-clay portion in water if the manufacturer recommends it.

Place it in a pot filled with loose, moist soil. Cover it with a thin layer of soil rather than burying it too deeply.

Keep the pot in a location with appropriate light and water it gently. The soil should remain moist but not waterlogged.

Germination depends on the type and quality of seeds, climate, season and storage conditions. Not every seed rakhi will successfully produce a plant, so reputable sellers should provide clear information about the seed type and planting method.

14

Make Your Own Eco-Friendly Rakhi

A handmade rakhi can be more meaningful than a commercially produced one.

Families can use materials already available at home, such as:

  • Cotton thread
  • Jute
  • Wool
  • Fabric scraps
  • Dried flowers
  • Leaves
  • Wooden beads
  • Coconut shell pieces
  • Recycled paper
  • Clay
  • Seeds
  • Old buttons

A simple rakhi can be made by braiding cotton or jute threads and attaching a small decoration made from recycled paper or fabric.

Children can draw a symbol, write a sibling’s initials or create a flower from folded paper. Adults can reuse embroidery thread, ribbons from old gifts or fabric left over from tailoring.

The goal is not perfection. A handmade rakhi carries the time and thought of the person who created it.

15

A Simple Seed-Paper Rakhi

To make a basic plantable rakhi, begin with recycled paper.

Tear the paper into small pieces and soak it in water. Blend or mash it into pulp. Mix in small seeds, taking care not to crush them.

Press the pulp into a small circular mold or shape it by hand. Allow it to dry naturally in the shade.

Once dry, make small holes on either side and attach cotton or jute thread.

Avoid painting the seed paper with chemical colors. Natural coloring can be created using turmeric, beetroot, flower petals or food-safe vegetable dyes.

After the festival, the central seed-paper portion can be planted.

16

Edible Rakhis

Another creative option is the edible rakhi.

These can be made from ingredients such as chocolate, dry fruits, fondant or traditional sweets. However, they should be prepared hygienically and stored properly, especially during warm and humid weather.

An edible rakhi should not be worn for an extended period, as it can collect dust and bacteria. It is better presented decoratively and eaten soon after the ritual.

Families should also consider allergies, dietary restrictions and sugar consumption.

Edible rakhis work best as a playful addition to the celebration rather than a complete replacement for a safe, wearable thread.

17

Reusable Rakhis

A reusable rakhi can become a bracelet, keychain, bookmark or piece of jewelry after the festival.

Fabric rakhis can be washed and preserved. Wooden or metal decorative pieces can be detached and reused. A rakhi with a small charm may become part of a bracelet or necklace.

Some families preserve rakhis in memory boxes along with photographs, letters and festival cards.

Reusability reduces waste and helps turn the rakhi into a lasting reminder of the relationship.

18

What to Look for When Buying an Eco-Friendly Rakhi

Not every product marketed as “green” is genuinely sustainable.

Before purchasing, consider the following questions:

  • Is the rakhi made from biodegradable or reusable material?
  • Does it contain plastic glitter, foam or synthetic decorations?
  • Are the seeds clearly identified?
  • Is the packaging recyclable or compostable?
  • Was the product made locally?
  • Does the seller provide planting instructions?
  • Are natural dyes used?
  • Is the rakhi durable enough to avoid breaking into small pieces?
  • Does the purchase support artisans, women’s groups or social enterprises?

A seed symbol printed on the packaging does not automatically make a product environmentally friendly. Transparent information is more valuable than vague green claims.

19

Supporting Artisans and Community Enterprises

Eco-friendly Raksha Bandhan can also support livelihoods.

Many handmade rakhis are produced by local artisans, women’s self-help groups, rural cooperatives, people with disabilities and nonprofit organizations.

Purchasing directly from such groups can provide income while preserving traditional skills. Handmade rakhis may use embroidery, block printing, natural fibers, clay work, beadwork or regional craft techniques.

Consumers should still verify claims where possible. Ethical purchasing means paying a fair price and understanding who benefits from the transaction.

The cheapest option is not always the most responsible one.

Chapter 05

Meaningful Celebration

Connection across distance, thoughtful gifts, emotional well-being and family pledges.

20

Digital Rakhis for Long-Distance Siblings

Families are increasingly spread across countries and time zones. A physical rakhi may not always arrive on time.

Digital rakhis, personalized videos, online cards and virtual ceremonies can help siblings celebrate across distance.

A sister may mail a rakhi in advance and then perform the ceremony over a video call. Siblings may create a shared photo album, record voice messages or watch childhood videos together.

Digital celebrations can also reduce shipping materials and transportation emissions, especially when paired with a thoughtful local action such as planting a tree or making a charitable donation.

The emotional value of Raksha Bandhan does not depend on physical proximity. What matters is intentional connection.

21

Meaningful Gifts Instead of Excessive Consumption

Gift-giving is an enjoyable part of Raksha Bandhan, but it does not need to become a competition.

A meaningful gift can respond to a sibling’s actual needs or values.

Examples include:

  • A handwritten letter
  • A framed childhood photograph
  • A book
  • A plant
  • A shared experience
  • A contribution toward education
  • A health checkup
  • A donation to a cause
  • Assistance with a personal project
  • A promise to spend regular time together

Some siblings may decide not to exchange material gifts at all. Instead, they may contribute jointly to a family goal or social cause.

The strongest gift is often evidence that one sibling has genuinely listened to the other.

22

Raksha Bandhan and Emotional Well-Being

Sibling relationships can be loving, but they can also be complicated.

Competition, unequal treatment, old resentments, inheritance disputes, family pressure and differences in lifestyle can create distance.

Raksha Bandhan should not require people to pretend that every relationship is harmonious.

For some siblings, the festival may be an opportunity to reconnect. For others, it may be a time to acknowledge pain, establish boundaries or choose not to participate.

Inclusion also means respecting people whose sibling relationships are estranged, abusive or emotionally unsafe. No ritual should obligate someone to resume contact without accountability or change.

A healthy celebration is based on consent, sincerity and respect.

23

Creating a Family Raksha Pledge

Families can make Raksha Bandhan more meaningful by creating a short mutual pledge.

Each participant can identify one commitment for the coming year.

For example:

“I will call you at least once a month.”

“I will support your career decision even if it is different from what I would choose.”

“I will share responsibility for our parents.”

“I will not dismiss your concerns.”

“I will be honest without being disrespectful.”

“I will respect your privacy.”

“I will celebrate your success.”

These promises may appear simple, but they bring the values of Raksha Bandhan into everyday life.

24

A Festival That Continues to Grow

Traditions survive not because they remain frozen, but because each generation finds meaningful ways to practice them.

Raksha Bandhan has moved from the protective amulet to the sibling thread, from royal legends to family rituals, and from biological relationships to chosen bonds.

Its modern forms—sister-to-sister rakhis, pet-friendly celebrations, plantable threads and mutual sibling pledges—are not necessarily departures from tradition.

They are expressions of the same enduring idea:

A relationship becomes sacred when people accept responsibility for one another.

The thread may be cotton, silk, seed paper or jute. It may be tied between a brother and sister, two sisters, two brothers, friends or members of a chosen family.

Its deepest meaning remains the promise behind it.

25

Conclusion: A More Inclusive Thread

Modern Raksha Bandhan is becoming less about who is expected to protect whom and more about how people care for one another.

It celebrates sisters who have been protectors, brothers who offer emotional support, friends who become family, pets who bring unconditional affection and individuals who choose to protect the natural world.

An inclusive Raksha Bandhan does not discard the traditional brother-sister ceremony. It allows that tradition to exist alongside newer expressions of love, equality and responsibility.

A green Raksha Bandhan also reminds us that celebrations should not create unnecessary harm. A sacred thread honoring protection should ideally protect the environment as well.

Ultimately, the most meaningful rakhi is not the most expensive, elaborate or fashionable one.

It is the one connected to an honest promise:

“

I respect you. I value our relationship. I will support you, and I will allow you to support me.

Uncracking Easter Traditions

From chocolate bunnies to painted eggs, Easter is a vibrant tapestry of pagan festivals, ancient folklore, and religious observance. Explore the interactive history of how these symbols came to be.

Why Are Eggs Used on Easter?

The egg is arguably the most recognizable symbol of Easter. But why did a simple breakfast staple become the centerpiece of a global holiday? The answer lies in a mix of practicality, ancient nature worship, and religious metaphor.

🌱

Ancient Fertility

Long before Christianity, eggs were a primary symbol of new life, fertility, and rebirth in pagan spring festivals. They represented the earth bursting with life after the cold, dead winter.

🍽️

The Lenten Fast

Historically, Christians abstained from eating eggs and meat during the 40 days of Lent. To prevent eggs from spoiling, people boiled and preserved them. Easter Sunday became a feast day to finally eat them.

✝️

The Empty Tomb

The Christian church adopted the egg as a metaphor for the resurrection. Just as a bird breaks out of its shell to begin its life, the egg represents Jesus emerging from the tomb.

The Story of the Easter Bunny

Rabbits do not lay eggs, so how did a bunny become the primary distributor of Easter treats? The journey begins in Europe and travels across the Atlantic.

1

The Goddess Eostre

Pagan Anglo-Saxons worshipped Eostre, the goddess of spring and dawn. Her earthly symbol was the hare, known for its rapid reproduction rates, cementing the rabbit's association with spring fertility.

2

The German Osterhase

By the 1700s, German folklore introduced the "Osterhase" or "Oschter Haws," an egg-laying hare. Children would build nests in which this creature could lay its colored eggs if they were good.

3

Journey to America

German immigrants settling in Pennsylvania brought the Osterhase tradition with them. Over time, the custom spread across the U.S., evolving into the modern Easter Bunny who brings chocolates and toys in baskets instead of nests.

🐰

The Paschal Lamb

Of all Easter symbols, the lamb is perhaps the most heavily rooted in religion. It connects directly to the Jewish holiday of Passover, where a lamb was sacrificed. In Christianity, Jesus is referred to as the "Lamb of God" who sacrificed himself for humanity's sins.

Cow Worship on Sankranti & Pongal

Across India’s harvest celebrations, cows and bulls are honored for their role in agriculture and daily life. During Makar Sankranti (Uttarayan) and the Pongal festival (especially Mattu Pongal), families perform Go Puja / cattle worship to express gratitude and pray for prosperity.

Why cattle are honored

Cows and bulls support farming through milk, manure used as natural fertilizer, and traditional field work. Worship is a cultural way of acknowledging their contribution to the harvest cycle.

Go Puja traditions

Families clean and decorate cattle, offer food, apply turmeric/kumkum, tie garlands, and seek blessings for health, prosperity, and a strong next season’s yield.

Sankranti & Pongal

Makar Sankranti marks the sun’s transition and seasonal shift. In Tamil Nadu, Pongal is celebrated over multiple days, with Mattu Pongal dedicated to cattle worship.

Cow Worship on Sankranti: Reasons & Meaning

On Sankranti, cows are worshipped in many regions—especially in rural communities—because of their practical importance to farming and their symbolic place in Hindu culture.

Theme Details
Agricultural significance Cows produce milk, dung serves as organic fertilizer, and oxen assist with ploughing and field tasks.
Religious importance In Hindu customs, cows are linked to gods and are seen as symbols of fertility and prosperity.
Gratitude & prosperity Farmers pray to show appreciation and ask for blessings for a prosperous harvest and the health of their families.
Cultural rituals Typical customs involve washing cattle, adorning them with garlands or colors, serving special meals, and holding communal parades.

Simply put, the reverence of cows on Sankranti is a communal gesture of gratitude towards the creatures that support farming, along with prayers for prosperity.

Cow Worship in Pongal: Mattu Pongal (Maatu Pongal)

In Tamil Nadu, the harvest festival Pongal cattle worship is central to and takes place over multiple days Mattu Pongal (the “cattle day”). While Sankranti is widely known for the sun’s transition and harvest gratitude, Mattu Pongal places farm animals at the heart of the celebration—recognizing the everyday partnership between people, land, and cattle.

Typical rituals

  • Bathing and grooming cattle; cleaning the cattle shed.
  • Decorating horns with colors, tying garlands, and placing bells/ornaments.
  • Applying turmeric and kumkum; offering prayers and incense.
  • Feeding special food (often including sweet pongal, fodder, and fruits).
  • Community gatherings where decorated cattle are led around the village.

Meaning & values

  • Gratitude: acknowledging cattle for ploughing, transport, milk, and manure.
  • Prosperity: prayers for healthy animals and productive farms.
  • Stewardship: reminding communities to care for animals and the environment.
  • Social cohesion: shared rituals that connect families and neighborhoods.

Manakrsankranti and Pongal context

“Makar Sankranti” and “Pongal” often fall in the same seasonal window and share a common theme: harvest gratitude. In many homes, the cow (and bull) is treated as a respected member of the farm ecosystem—so cattle worship becomes a natural, visible way to celebrate the harvest season across regions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is cow worship only part of Pongal?

No. Cow and cattle worship occurs in several harvest-related traditions, including Makar Sankranti celebrations in different regions. Pongal’s Mattu Pongal is one of the most explicit “cattle day” observances.

Why are bulls also honored?

Bulls and oxen have historically provided field labor, transport, and support for agricultural livelihoods. In many communities, they are honored alongside cows as part of the same gratitude tradition.

What should visitors do respectfully?

Follow local custom, ask before photographing animals/rituals, avoid interrupting prayers or processions, and treat decorated cattle calmly and with space.

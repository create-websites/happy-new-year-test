The Heart of the Celebration

Date and Meaning

**Mother's Day** is celebrated each year on the **second Sunday of May** in the U.S., Canada, and many other countries. The day is dedicated to honoring mothers, **motherhood, maternal bonds**, and the vital role of mothers in society. Unlike older traditional holidays, the modern U.S. Mother's Day was designed as a day of sincere, personal gratitude rather than lavish festivities. It’s a time for thoughtful gestures, heartfelt notes, and making mothers feel valued, often involving **brunches** and the gift of **flowers**.

The Founder Who Fought Commercialism

Anna Jarvis and the Anti-Holiday Campaign

Mother's Day, as we know it, was founded by **Anna Jarvis** in the early 1900s to honor her mother, a peace activist. After a successful campaign, it became a national holiday in **1914**, declared by President Woodrow Wilson. Jarvis intended the day to be personal, with children writing heartfelt letters of love and thanks. Sadly, she later fought against its commercialization, opposing industries like greeting cards and flowers for turning it into a profit-driven occasion instead of a sincere celebration.

Core Traditions of Celebration

These customs reflect the essence of service and gratitude celebrated on Mother's Day globally.

1. Wearing Carnations

A tradition by Anna Jarvis: **white carnations** signify a late mother, while **pink or red carnations** celebrate a living one, symbolizing love and purity.

2. The Brunch/Breakfast

Preparing a meal for Mom, like breakfast in bed or brunch out, is a popular gesture, representing a break from her daily chores.

3. Sentimental Cards

Be it store-bought or handmade, the card holds importance. The focus stays on conveying sincere thanks in a lasting, written way.

4. Pampering Gifts

Presents often emphasize comfort and pampering, like spa kits, aromatherapy candles, cozy apparel, or accessories.

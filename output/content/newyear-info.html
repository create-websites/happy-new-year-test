A Journey Through Time

New Year's Day hasn't always been January 1st. Its history weaves together agriculture, astronomy, and politics, shaping the calendar we know today.

c. 2000 BCE: Babylonian Akitu

The first known New Year festival, Akitu, took place in Mesopotamia. Linked to the spring equinox, this 11-day event honored nature's renewal and reinforced the king's sacred authority.

45 BCE: Rome & The Julian Calendar

Julius Caesar implemented a solar calendar, setting January 1st as New Year's Day. The month honored Janus, the two-faced god symbolizing reflection on the past and anticipation of the future.

1582 CE: The Gregorian Reform

Pope Gregory XIII fixed the Julian calendar's flaws by creating the Gregorian calendar, now used worldwide and establishing January 1st as the common New Year‚Äôs Day.
